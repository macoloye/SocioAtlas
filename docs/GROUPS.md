# 🗂️ GROUPS.md — Dynamic Stance Groups

> Stance groups are **not predefined**. They are generated fresh for each simulation based on the event. The system looks at the event and asks: "who are the natural camps that would form around this?" — then populates each camp with agents.

---

## The Core Idea

For any event, society doesn't split along professional lines — it splits along **stake lines**. The groups that form depend entirely on what the event is:

| Event Type | Groups That Naturally Form |
|---|---|
| Product launch | Early adopters / Skeptical buyers / Uninterested consumers / Competitors |
| Protest | Core supporters / Sympathetic bystanders / Opposed for reason A / Opposed for reason B / Indifferent public |
| Policy announcement | Direct beneficiaries / Those who pay the cost / Ideological allies / Ideological opponents / Uninformed public |
| Corporate scandal | Outraged customers / Loyal defenders / Opportunistic rivals / Indifferent shareholders |
| Scientific breakthrough | Excited adopters / Cautious skeptics / Threatened incumbents / Regulatory watchdogs |

The groups are **generated by the LLM** as the first step of each simulation run — before any agents are assigned stances.

---

## Group Generation Rules

When generating groups for a new event, apply these constraints:

1. **3 to 6 groups per event.** Fewer than 3 collapses real diversity. More than 6 creates redundancy.
2. **Each group is defined by its relationship to the event** — not by demographics, profession, or ideology.
3. **Groups should cover the full stance spectrum** — at least one group with reason to support, one with reason to oppose, and at least one ambiguous or context-dependent group.
4. **Groups can overlap in real life** — a person can be a "concerned parent" and a "brand loyalist" simultaneously. Groups capture the *lens*, not the whole person.
5. **Name groups in plain language** — describe what they are to this event, not what they are in general.

---

## Group Schema

Each generated group has:

```yaml
group_id: string                  # snake_case identifier
name: string                      # plain language, event-specific
description: string               # one sentence: who are these people and why do they care?
stance_posture: string            # their default orientation to this event
primary_incentive: M | P | I | S | N   # dominant incentive type (see INCENTIVES.md)
activation_stage: T1 | T2 | T3 | T4   # when they typically enter the conversation
agents: [Agent]                   # 3–8 personas drawn from this group
```

## Illustrative Examples

> These are examples only — real groups are generated per event, not selected from a list.

---

### Example A — Product Launch: A new AI assistant app

```yaml
- group_id: power_users
  name: Early Adopter Power Users
  description: Tech-forward people who immediately try new tools and form strong opinions
  stance_posture: Likely to support, will critique specific features
  primary_incentive: M (productivity gain)
  activation_stage: T1
  agents:
    - "A productivity-obsessed freelance designer who beta-tests every new app"
    - "A software developer who evaluates tools for potential workflow integration"
    - "A content creator who is always searching for an edge over competitors"

- group_id: privacy_skeptics
  name: Privacy-Concerned Skeptics
  description: People who distrust tech companies collecting data and resist AI adoption
  stance_posture: Likely to oppose or demand transparency
  primary_incentive: S (personal data as threat)
  activation_stage: T2
  agents:
    - "A cybersecurity professional who scrutinizes every new app's data practices"
    - "A digital rights activist who sees AI assistants as surveillance infrastructure"
    - "A parent concerned about what an AI app does with their family's conversations"

- group_id: casual_browsers
  name: Casually Curious Public
  description: People who hear about it through feeds and form a vague impression
  stance_posture: Neutral, stance depends on what narrative reaches them
  primary_incentive: I (social signal — does using this make me look good or bad?)
  activation_stage: T3
  agents:
    - "A mid-career professional who installs trending apps but rarely commits to them"
    - "A college student who downloads it because their friends did"

- group_id: incumbent_threatened
  name: Threatened Incumbents
  description: Companies or professionals whose market position this product challenges
  stance_posture: Likely to oppose or publicly downplay
  primary_incentive: M (competitive threat)
  activation_stage: T2
  agents:
    - "A product manager at a rival tech company assessing competitive risk"
    - "A freelance writer who sees AI writing tools as a threat to their income"
```

### Example B — Protest: Anti-government corruption march

```yaml
- group_id: core_protesters
  name: Core March Participants
  description: People who show up, carry signs, and identify with the movement's cause
  stance_posture: Strongly support
  primary_incentive: N (moral conviction) + I (movement identity)
  activation_stage: T1
  agents:
    - "A young civic organizer who has been building toward this moment for months"
    - "A retired teacher who has watched institutions decay over decades"
    - "A first-time protester motivated by a specific recent incident"

- group_id: sympathetic_nonparticipants
  name: Sympathetic But Passive Observers
  description: Agree with the cause but didn't march — watching and signaling support online
  stance_posture: Support, but low intensity
  primary_incentive: I (moral alignment without personal cost)
  activation_stage: T3
  agents:
    - "A professional who agrees with the cause but fears career risk from visible participation"
    - "A suburban resident who shared the event on social media but didn't attend"

- group_id: law_order_opposed
  name: Order-and-Stability Opponents
  description: Oppose the protest not because they support corruption, but because they fear disruption
  stance_posture: Oppose — but on process, not on the underlying grievance
  primary_incentive: S (social order as survival)
  activation_stage: T2
  agents:
    - "A small business owner whose street was blocked during the march"
    - "A conservative voter who distrusts street protest as a political tool"
    - "A police union representative focused on officer safety and public order"

- group_id: politically_opposed
  name: Ideological Opponents
  description: Oppose the protest because they support the government being targeted
  stance_posture: Strongly oppose
  primary_incentive: P (protecting their political coalition)
  activation_stage: T1
  agents:
    - "A ruling party loyalist who reads every protest as a foreign-funded destabilization attempt"
    - "A state media journalist who frames the protesters as radical agitators"

- group_id: disengaged_public
  name: Disengaged Majority
  description: Heard about it, formed no strong view, waiting to see if it matters to their life
  stance_posture: Neutral
  primary_incentive: none active
  activation_stage: T3–T4
  agents:
    - "A commuter who walked past the protest and went home"
    - "A news-fatigued person who scrolled past coverage without engaging"
```

## Agent Persona Format

Each agent inside a group is a **single sentence** describing their identity and behavioral logic as it relates to the event:

```
✅ "A freelance designer who beta-tests every new app and posts honest reviews"
✅ "A small business owner whose street was blocked during the march"
✅ "A ruling party loyalist who reads every protest as a foreign-funded attack"

❌ "A 34-year-old marketing professional with progressive political views" (too generic)
❌ "A person who is interested in technology" (no behavioral logic)
❌ "A conservative" (identity label without event-specific logic)
```

The sentence should answer: **why does this specific person care about this specific event?**
